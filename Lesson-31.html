Vocabulary Lesson-31

Privilege noun ( /ˈprɪv.əl.ɪdʒ/) विशेषाधिकार/सुविधा/शौभाग्य an advantage that only one person or group of people has, usually because of their position or because they are rich: 1- Healthcare should be a right, not a privilege. 2-Senior management enjoys certain privileges, such as company cars and health insurance. An opportunity to do something special or enjoyable: 1-I had the privilege of interviewing the Indian prime minister. 2- It was a real privilege to meet her. 3- I have had the privilege of analyzing several thousand men and women, 98% of whom were classed as “failures.”

Radically adverb /ˈræd.ɪ.kəl.i/ Completely or extremely: 1- The health service must be radically reformed. 2- They decided to try a radically different approach. 3-Car styles have changed radically in the past 20 years. 3-There is something radically wrong with the system of education, which permits 90% of the people to go through life as failures.

Vocabulary

Outstanding adj (/ˌaʊtˈstæn.dɪŋ/) (EXCELLENT). Clearly very much better than what is usual: 1-His performance was outstanding. 2- The work is satisfactory but not outstanding. 3-No person may enjoy outstanding success without good health.

 Emulate verb ( /ˈem.jə.leɪt/) To copy something achieved by someone else and try to do it as well as they have: or to copy someone’s behavior or try to be like someone else because you admire or respect that person:1 – We emulate those with whom we associate most closely (हम उन लोगों का अनुकरण करते हैं जिनके साथ हम सबसे अधिक निकटता से जुड़ते हैं।). 2- They hope to emulate the success of other software companies.

Procrastinate verb (/proʊˈkræs.tə.neɪt/) देर करना/टालना/आजकल करना To keep delaying something that must be done, often because it is unpleasant or boring: 1-He procrastinates until it is too late. 2- People often procrastinate when it comes to paperwork. 3-Sir: Next time, I won’t procrastinate.

Conquer verb ( /ˈkɑŋ·kər/) To deal with or successfully fight against a problem or an unreasonable fear: 1-Energy and persistence conquer all things.(ऊर्जा और जिद सभी चीज़ों को जीत लेती हैं।). 2-You must conquer your fear. (आपको अपने डर पर विजय प्राप्त करनी चाहिए). 3-The only way to conquer a fear is to face it.

Vocabulary

Promptly adverb (/ˈprɑːmpt.li/) quickly, without delay, or at the arranged time: 1-He paid the money back promptly. 2-He handles matters promptly. 3-A reply came very promptly.

Urge verb/noun (/ɝːdʒ/) To strongly advise or try to persuade someone to do a particular thing: 1- The Police are urging anyone who saw the accident to contact them immediately. 2-She urged him to stay. 3-The charity urged the government to reconsider its decision. A strong wish, especially one that is difficult or impossible to control: 1-The urge was beyond her control. 2-I can never resist the urge to laugh. 3-I had a sudden urge to slap him in the face.

Attain verb /əˈteɪn/ प्राप्त करना To reach or succeed in getting something: 1-He has attained the highest grade in his music exams. 2-We need to identify the best ways of attaining our objectives/goals. 3-The cheetah can attain speeds of up to 97 kph. 4-Here is your opportunity to attain a goal.

Present Perfect Tense lesson-31

Unspecified time before the present time.
We use the Present Perfect to say that an action happened at an unspecified time before now. The Present Perfect cannot be used with time expressions that indicate a specific time; yesterday, a year ago, when I was younger, etc. We can use the Present Perfect with; ever, never, already, yet, etc.

  • I have traveled to England many times.
  • Have you read the book yet?
  • We have met before.

Here are some topics that use the Present Perfect:

Experience
Use the Present Perfect to talk about experience: (remember the Present Perfect cannot be used with a specific event)

  • I have been to Germany. (in my life)
  • I have never been to France.
  • I have never been skiing.
  • I have studied English for several years now.

Change over time
Hence, the Present Perfect can express change over time:

  • You’ve changed since I saw you last.
  • He has become more concerned about his social status.
  • Travel has turned into a huge industry in the last few decades.

Achievements

  • Science has advanced in leaps and bounds.
  • Man has accomplished great things.
  • He has done well in finance.

Uncompleted actions

  • I have not yet finished the report.
  • The rain hasn’t stopped.
  • They haven’t yet arrived.

Several actions

Therefore the Present Perfect can refer to several actions which have occurred at different times in the past but it suggests that the process is incomplete.

  • We have had three power cuts this week.
  • The team has experienced several setbacks so far.
  • John has sat for three tests this month.

Time expressions

Sometimes we want to limit the time we are looking at when we talk about experiences. This is done by using certain time expressions:

  • Have you been abroad this year?
  • I have read three books by the same author in the last month.
  • Peter has worked for three different companies since he graduated two years ago.

From the past until now

We show that something has started in the past and has continued up until now with non-continuous verbs by using the Present Perfect. Phrases that show duration can be used; for three weeks, since Monday, for fifteen minutes, etc.

  • I have had a cold for two weeks.
  • He has been here for six months.
  • I have waited for too long.

Position of adverbs

The position of adverbs such as; always, never, only, still, just, etc. are placed after the auxiliary verb and before the past participle:

  • I’ve just seen Sarah.
  • I have only spoken to her over the phone.
