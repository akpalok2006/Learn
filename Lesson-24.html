Vocabulary

Afflict verb /əˈflɪkt/ सताना/पीड़ा देना/दु:ख देना/कष्ट देनाIf a problem or illness afflicts a person or thing, they suffer from it: 1-He was afflicted with severe asthma. 2-I don’t want to afflict you with my troubles. 3-You shall not afflict any widow or fatherless child. 4-I wish you wouldn’t afflict me with your constant complaints. (Lesson-24)

Dispel verb /dɪˈspel/ दूर करना/हटाना to remove fears, doubts, or false ideas, usually by proving them wrong or unnecessary: 1-We hope the program will dispel certain misconceptions about the disease. 2-It was important to dispel the myth that Aids was a gay disease. 3- You should dispel your doubt first.

Gust verb/noun /ɡʌst/ झोंका A sudden strong wind: 1-A sudden gust of wind blew his umbrella inside out. 2- Winds are gusting up to 55 miles per hour.

Admit verb /ədˈmɪt/ स्वीकार करना/मान लेना To agree that something is true, especially unwillingly: 1-He admitted his guilt/mistake. 2-At first he denied stealing the money, but he later admitted (to) it. 3- I wasn’t entirely honest with him, I admit.

Eloquently adv /ˈel.ə.kwənt.li/ अर्थपूर्ण In a way that gives a strong, clear message: 1-He spoke eloquently. 2-She argued her point eloquently and persuasively.

Catalyst noun /ˈkætəlɪst/ Someone or something that causes a big change: 1-True decisions are the catalyst for turning our dreams into reality. 2-I believe in stories as a catalyst for change. 3-The book reading was a catalyst for my life change.

Condescending adjective /ˌkɑːn.dəˈsen.dɪŋ/ showing that you consider yourself better or more intelligent: 1-His condescending attitude offended his teammates. 2-I hate the way he’s so condescending to his staff!

Absurd adjective /əbˈsɝːd/ बेतुका,बेतुका Stupid and unreasonable, or silly in a humorous way: 1-What an absurd thing to say! (क्या बेतुकी बात है!) 2-Do I look absurd in this hat?

Put on take off clothes

In English, we use “put on”, “wear”, and “take off” for any piece of clothing (hats, shirts, pants, shoes, socks, etc)

To put on clothes: To dress oneself, to get dressed. 1- I put on clothes. 2- I have put on clothes.

To take off clothes: To undress oneself, to get undressed. 1- I take off clothes. 2-I took off clothes. 3-I have taken off clothes

On the fly: If you do something on the fly, you do it quickly, often while you are doing something else, without preparing and without thinking too much about how it should be done: -This new rule seems to have been created on the fly. 2-Sometimes you have to make decisions on the fly. 3- My wife is always on the fly. 4-I usually eat breakfast on the fly.

Past Progressive (Continuous) Tense

The past progressive tense is used to demonstrate an action that was happening in the past for a period of time in a particular context. The context can be a specific time or another action. (Lesson-24)
