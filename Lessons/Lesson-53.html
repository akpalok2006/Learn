Also, As well, Even, Too, Either, Neither

We use also in the normal mid position for adverbs, between the subject and main verb, after the modal verb or first auxiliary verb, or after be as a main verb.

  • There is also a golf course five miles away.
  • We also went to see the film.
  • No answer is also an answer.

As well is much more common in speaking than in writing, and is more common in speaking than also. As well almost always comes in the end position

  • He speaks English and Spanish as well.
  • Her novels are well-written and commercial as well.
  • And I’ll have the mixed vegetables as well.

We usually put too in the end position:

  • I cannot be your friend and your flatterer too.
  • Gill’s having chicken. I’ll probably have chicken too.
  • She looks really tired and she must be really hungry too.

We use either at the end of a negative sentece.

  • I don’t eat meat and my husband doesn’t either.
  • Those who can’t lose will never win either.
  • I do not smoke and he does not either.

When even referring to a whole clause or sentence, we usually put it in the normal mid position for adverbs, between the subject and the main verb, after the modal verb or first auxiliary verb, or after be as a main verb:

  • I haven’t even started making dinner.
  • She didn’t even call to say she wasn’t coming.
  • She didn’t even have the strength to stand up.

We can put even or not even before the part of the clause or sentence we want to focus on:

  • Even a five-year-old can do it easily.
  • Even I can speak English.
  • Even she knows how to cook.

We use neither + auxiliary/modal verb + subject to mean ‘also not’:

  • I do not speak English. Neither does she.
  • She does not go for the evening walk. Neither do I.
  • You don’t like this book. Neither do I.
